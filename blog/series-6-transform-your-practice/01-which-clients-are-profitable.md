# The R40,000 Question: Which of Your Clients Are Actually Profitable?

*This is Part 1 of "Run a Leaner Practice with Kazi" — practical insights for firm owners who want to work smarter, not just harder.*

---

You billed R40,000 last month. Good month. But how much of that was profit?

If you're like most small accounting firms, you don't actually know. You know what you billed. You know what you paid in salaries. But do you know which clients made you money and which ones quietly ate your margin?

The client who calls every Friday with "just a quick question" — is that covered by their fee, or are you donating two hours a week? The annual tax return that should take 8 hours but somehow takes 14 every year — are you billing for 14 or eating the difference?

Most firms don't answer these questions because the math is hard. You'd need to track hours per client, multiply by each team member's cost rate, compare to what you actually billed, and do it for every client, every month.

Kazi does this automatically.

## What Profitability Actually Looks Like

When your team logs time against engagements — which they're doing anyway for billing — Kazi captures two numbers per entry:

- **Billing rate**: what the client pays for that hour (R1,500 for the partner, R850 for a senior, R450 for a junior)
- **Cost rate**: what that hour actually costs your firm (salary + overhead, roughly R600 for the partner, R350 for a senior, R180 for a junior)

The difference is your margin. And Kazi shows it at every level:

**Per engagement**: "The Q1 tax return for Thornton Properties used 14 hours. Revenue: R12,600. Cost: R5,040. Margin: R7,560 (60%)."

**Per client**: "Thornton Properties generated R156,000 in the last 12 months. Your cost to serve them: R62,400. Margin: R93,600 (60%)." But next to them: "Van der Merwe Trust generated R48,000. Your cost: R41,200. Margin: R6,800 (14%)." The Van der Merwe account *looks* profitable. It barely is.

**Per team member**: "Sipho billed 142 hours last month at an average rate of R850. His cost rate is R350. Firm contribution: R71,000." Versus: "Lerato billed 95 hours at R450 average. Cost: R180. Contribution: R25,650." Sipho is your revenue engine. Lerato might need higher-value work.

**Per firm**: "This quarter, firm-wide margin is 52%. Last quarter it was 58%. The drop is driven by three clients whose scope expanded without fee adjustments."

## The Uncomfortable Truths

Most firms that start tracking profitability discover two things:

**1. Their best clients aren't who they think.** The big-name client with the R20,000 monthly retainer feels important. But if they consume 30 hours of your team's time each month, your effective rate is R667/hour — below your senior's billing rate. Meanwhile, the quiet client paying R4,000/month who needs 3 hours of work is earning you R1,333/hour effective.

**2. They're under-billing their most complex work.** Annual financial statements for a holding company with 4 subsidiaries always takes longer than estimated. But the firm quotes a fixed fee based on the "standard" engagement, not the actual complexity. Over 3 years, the cumulative under-billing adds up to a full month of unbilled work.

You can't fix what you can't see. And you can't see these patterns without tracking time against cost rates — which requires software, not spreadsheets.

## Rate Cards That Match Reality

Kazi uses a three-level rate hierarchy:

**Firm defaults**: Every team member has a standard billing rate and cost rate. Partner: R1,500 billing / R600 cost. Senior: R850 / R350. Article clerk: R450 / R180.

**Client overrides**: Long-standing clients might get discounted rates. "For Van der Merwe Trust, the partner rate is R1,200 instead of R1,500." The override is explicit and visible — not buried in a fee quote from 2019.

**Engagement overrides**: A specific project might have special pricing. "The due diligence engagement for Thornton Properties uses premium rates because it's outside scope." The engagement override takes priority over the client rate and firm default.

When someone logs time, Kazi resolves the correct rate automatically: engagement rate > client rate > firm default. The team doesn't think about rates — they log hours. The system handles the math.

## What Changes When You Can See

Firms that track profitability by client start making different decisions:

**Fee reviews happen with data, not gut feel.** Instead of "I think we should raise Van der Merwe's fees," it's "Van der Merwe's margin is 14%. Our firm average is 52%. If we raise their fee by R8,000/month, they're still getting good value and our margin normalizes."

**Scope creep becomes visible.** When the Q1 tax return budget is 8 hours and the actuals are 14, the budget tracker turns amber at 80% and red at 100%. The partner sees it before the engagement is done — not after the invoice goes out short.

**Team allocation improves.** If your R1,500/hour partner is doing work that an R450/hour article clerk could handle, the profitability report shows it: "Partner spent 12 hours on bookkeeping entries for 3 clients." That's R12,600 of partner time on R5,400 of work.

**Dormant clients surface automatically.** Kazi flags clients with no activity for 180 days. Not as a "delete this client" warning — as a "this client hasn't been touched in 6 months, is the engagement still active?" nudge. Dormant clients with active retainers are revenue leaks.

## The Numbers Nobody Tracks

Ask yourself these questions. If you can't answer them in under 30 seconds, you're running blind:

- What's your firm's average margin per billable hour?
- Which 3 clients consumed the most partner time last quarter?
- What percentage of your logged hours are billable vs. non-billable?
- Which team member has the highest utilization rate?
- How many hours did you bill last month that were at a discounted rate?

These aren't nice-to-know metrics. They're the difference between a firm that grows profitably and one that grows revenue while margin shrinks.

Kazi shows all of them on the dashboard — updated in real time as your team logs work.
